### Bloom's Taxonomy — Cognitive Levels Applied to 7th Grade Algebra
@@@meta:source https://www.bloomstaxonomy.net/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-001

Bloom's Taxonomy defines six hierarchical cognitive levels that govern how students acquire and internalize knowledge: Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, and Create. For 7th grade algebra instruction — specifically linear equations of the form y = mx + b — students typically enter at the Remember and Understand levels, recalling arithmetic rules and beginning to interpret what slope and intercept mean conceptually. A Socratic tutor must identify which cognitive level a student is operating at before selecting a hint or question strategy. Asking an Evaluate-level question to a student still at the Remember level produces confusion and disengagement. The tutor should scaffold upward one level at a time: first confirm the student can recall what m and b represent, then confirm they understand why changing m changes the steepness, then prompt them to apply this to a new equation. The six levels map directly to hint tier selection: Tier 1 hints target Understanding, Tier 2 hints target Application, and Tier 3 worked parallels target Analysis. A student who can Analyze slope behavior but fails on Evaluation tasks (e.g., determining which of two lines is steeper from equations alone) needs targeted practice at that transition, not remediation at lower levels. The taxonomy also informs profile mastery scoring: a student demonstrating Apply-level competence consistently should receive a mastery score of 0.6–0.75, while Analyze-level fluency warrants 0.75–0.90.

### Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development — Calibrating Tutor Intervention
@@@meta:source https://www.simplypsychology.org/vygotsky.html
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-002

Lev Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) defines the cognitive space between what a student can accomplish independently and what they can accomplish with expert guidance. For AI tutoring systems, the ZPD is operationalized through the learning profile's mastery matrix: a concept with mastery score below 0.3 is outside the student's current ZPD and should not be introduced without extensive scaffolding from prerequisite concepts. A concept with mastery score between 0.3 and 0.7 sits within the ZPD and is the optimal target for Socratic questioning — the student has enough foundation to be guided forward but cannot solve without support. A concept above 0.7 is approaching independent mastery and requires only light confirmation prompts. The tutor must never operate outside the student's ZPD by assigning problems that require knowledge the mastery matrix shows as unscored or zero. For y = mx + b specifically, the ZPD prerequisite chain is: integer arithmetic → ratio and proportion → coordinate plane navigation → slope as rate of change → y-intercept as starting value → full linear equation interpretation. If a student's mastery matrix shows coordinate plane navigation below 0.4, the tutor must redirect there before engaging slope concepts. Scaffolding within the ZPD is the mechanism by which the tutor's Socratic hints become effective rather than frustrating.

### Gardner's Multiple Intelligences — Cognitive Fingerprint Mapping for Algebra
@@@meta:source https://www.pz.harvard.edu/projects/multiple-intelligences
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-003

Howard Gardner's Theory of Multiple Intelligences identifies at least eight distinct cognitive strengths that influence how individuals best acquire and retain information: Linguistic, Logical-Mathematical, Spatial, Musical, Bodily-Kinesthetic, Interpersonal, Intrapersonal, and Naturalist. For a 7th grade algebra tutor, three intelligences are directly relevant to profile construction and hint routing. Logical-Mathematical learners engage best with symbolic notation, formal rules, and deductive reasoning — these students respond well to equation-first presentation of y = mx + b and can handle abstract manipulation early. Spatial learners engage best with graphs, coordinate planes, and visual representations of slope as physical steepness — these students require graph-first anchoring before symbolic notation is introduced. Linguistic learners engage best when mathematical concepts are embedded in narrative or verbal explanation — for these students, framing slope as a story ("for every step you take to the right, how many steps do you go up?") produces faster comprehension than symbolic or visual approaches. The intake quiz must include scenario-based questions that identify which of these three primary intelligences dominates the student's mathematical processing. The cognitive fingerprint field primaryLearningMode maps directly to Gardner's categories: algebraic corresponds to Logical-Mathematical, visual corresponds to Spatial, and narrative corresponds to Linguistic. Hint phrasing must route through the dominant intelligence even when the mathematical content is identical.

### Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle — Session Structure and Reflection Timing
@@@meta:source https://www.simplypsychology.org/learning-kolb.html
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-004

David Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle describes four stages through which learners process new experiences: Concrete Experience (doing), Reflective Observation (reviewing), Abstract Conceptualization (concluding), and Active Experimentation (planning next steps). For a stateless AI tutor that cannot carry conversation history between sessions, Kolb's cycle provides the structural framework for how each individual session should be sequenced internally. A single tutoring session on slope should begin with a Concrete Experience — the student attempts a problem or observes a graph. The tutor then prompts Reflective Observation through Tier 1 Socratic questions ("What did you notice about the line when the number in front of x was bigger?"). Abstract Conceptualization is triggered by Tier 2 questions that help the student articulate a general rule. Active Experimentation is the student applying their stated rule to a new problem. The XML delta system should capture at which Kolb stage the student successfully transitioned: a student who consistently stalls at Reflective Observation has a specific friction pattern distinct from one who stalls at Abstract Conceptualization. These are different pedagogical problems requiring different hint strategies. Kolb also identified four learning styles derived from preferences within the cycle — Diverger, Assimilator, Converger, Accommodator — which map to the processingStyle field in the cognitive fingerprint: Assimilators and Convergers tend toward sequential processing while Divergers and Accommodators tend toward gestalt-first processing.

### Differentiated Instruction Theory — Profile-Driven Content Routing
@@@meta:source https://www.ascd.org/el/articles/differentiated-instruction
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-005

Differentiated Instruction (DI), formalized by Carol Ann Tomlinson, holds that effective teaching adjusts content, process, and product based on individual student readiness, interest, and learning profile. For an AI tutoring system, DI is the theoretical basis for why a single student profile drives fundamentally different tutoring behavior for the same mathematical content. Two students working on y = mx + b with identical mastery scores may require entirely different hint sequencing, question phrasing, and problem presentation based on their cognitive fingerprint. DI theory specifies three primary dimensions of differentiation relevant to the tutor engine. Content differentiation means the tutor selects which aspect of the linear equation to focus on first based on profile data — a spatial learner begins with graphing, a logical-mathematical learner begins with the equation structure. Process differentiation means the tutor's questioning strategy, hint tier progression speed, and use of analogies adapts to the student's processingStyle and frustrationThreshold. Product differentiation is less relevant for MVP but will matter at V2 when students demonstrate mastery through different output types. DI theory also establishes that pre-assessment — the intake quiz — is not optional scaffolding but a prerequisite to effective differentiation. Without baseline profile data, the tutor defaults to a generic approach that serves no student optimally. This is the theoretical justification for requiring profile completion before first tutor session access.

### Mastery Learning Theory — Threshold-Gated Concept Progression
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00405847509482733
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-006

Benjamin Bloom's Mastery Learning theory holds that virtually all students can achieve high levels of learning if given sufficient time and appropriate instruction, and that students should not advance to new concepts until demonstrating mastery of prerequisite concepts above a defined threshold. For the tutor engine's mastery matrix, Mastery Learning provides the theoretical basis for threshold-gated progression. A mastery score threshold of 0.75 (75% demonstrated competence across multiple interaction instances) should function as the gate between prerequisite concepts and dependent concepts in the y = mx + b concept chain. Students who attempt slope calculations before achieving 0.75 mastery on ratio and proportion consistently show higher error rates and lower retention — Bloom's original research demonstrated this across multiple subject domains. The mastery matrix's Bayesian Knowledge Tracing scoring system operationalizes Mastery Learning by providing a probabilistic estimate of true mastery rather than a simple percentage of correct answers, accounting for the possibility of lucky guesses and careless errors. Mastery Learning also informs the XML delta system's update logic: a single session's performance should not dramatically shift a mastery score in either direction. Bloom's research suggests that mastery assessment requires multiple independent demonstrations across different problem types and presentation formats before a score update above 0.15 per session is warranted.

### Constructivism — Why the Tutor Never Gives Answers
@@@meta:source https://www.learning-theories.com/constructivism.html
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-007

Constructivism, developed through the work of Piaget, Vygotsky, and Bruner, holds that learners construct knowledge actively through experience rather than receiving it passively through instruction. This is the foundational theoretical justification for the tutor's core behavioral constraint: never provide the answer. When a student receives a direct answer, the constructivist learning process — active struggle, hypothesis formation, error detection, and self-correction — is bypassed. The resulting knowledge is fragile, surface-level, and rapidly forgotten because it was never constructed by the learner's own cognitive apparatus. Constructivism further holds that prior knowledge is the scaffolding upon which new knowledge is built, which is why the cognitive fingerprint and mastery matrix injection at session start is not merely a personalization feature but a theoretical necessity. The tutor cannot guide a student toward constructing new knowledge without knowing what knowledge structures already exist in that student's understanding. For 7th grade algebra specifically, Bruner's spiral curriculum variant of constructivism is particularly applicable: slope and linear equations are revisited in increasingly abstract forms across grades 7 through 10. The tutor's role is not to teach y = mx + b once but to establish the conceptual foundation that all future encounters with linear relationships will build upon. This means prioritizing deep conceptual understanding over procedural fluency in all hint and question strategies.

### Social Learning Theory — Motivational Profile Calibration
@@@meta:source https://www.simplypsychology.org/bandura.html
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-008

Albert Bandura's Social Learning Theory, and specifically his concept of self-efficacy, holds that a student's belief in their own capability to perform a task is a stronger predictor of academic performance than actual ability. For the motivational profile's selfEfficacyLevel field, Bandura's research establishes that self-efficacy is domain-specific: a student may have high self-efficacy in reading but low self-efficacy in mathematics, and this math-specific self-efficacy is what the intake quiz and ongoing profile updates must measure and respond to. Low math self-efficacy produces avoidance behavior, premature help-seeking, and learned helplessness — all of which manifest in tutoring sessions as the student asking for the answer, disengaging after first failure, or expressing that they "just don't get math." The tutor must detect these behavioral patterns and respond with preferredReinforcementStyle calibration: students with low self-efficacy require frequent small-win acknowledgment and explicit attribution of success to their own effort rather than luck. Bandura identifies four sources of self-efficacy: mastery experiences (past successes), vicarious experiences (seeing similar others succeed), verbal persuasion (encouragement from credible sources), and physiological states (managing anxiety). The tutor can directly influence mastery experiences by calibrating problem difficulty to produce regular success, and verbal persuasion through reinforcement phrasing. The frictionMap's successfulWorkarounds field should track which reinforcement approaches have successfully increased engagement for a specific student.

### Cognitive Load Theory — Context Window Management and Hint Design
@@@meta:source https://www.instructionaldesign.org/theories/cognitive-load/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-009

Cognitive Load Theory, developed by John Sweller, holds that working memory has a finite capacity and that instructional design must manage the total cognitive load imposed on learners to prevent overload and breakdown. For a 7th grade algebra tutor, Cognitive Load Theory has direct implications for both the system prompt architecture and the hint design strategy. Sweller distinguishes three types of cognitive load: intrinsic load (complexity inherent to the material itself), extraneous load (complexity introduced by poor instructional design), and germane load (cognitive effort directed toward schema formation and learning). The tutor's system prompt must minimize extraneous cognitive load by presenting only the information relevant to the current problem — this is the theoretical basis for injecting only the mastery matrix entries relevant to today's concept rather than the full matrix. Injecting irrelevant profile data into the context increases the extraneous load on the LLM's reasoning and produces less focused Socratic questions. For hint design, Cognitive Load Theory mandates that each hint reduce the student's intrinsic load by decomposing the problem into sub-problems that fit within working memory limits. For 12-14 year old students, research indicates working memory capacity of approximately 4±1 chunks — meaning a problem that requires holding more than 5 distinct pieces of information simultaneously will produce errors even in students with adequate conceptual understanding. The workingMemoryLoad field in the cognitive fingerprint captures this individual variation, and the tutor must adjust the complexity of each hint's sub-problem accordingly.

### Fixed vs. Growth Mindset Theory — Failure Response Calibration
@@@meta:source https://www.mindsetonline.com/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-learningstyles-010

Carol Dweck's Mindset Theory distinguishes between fixed mindset (the belief that intelligence and ability are static traits) and growth mindset (the belief that ability develops through effort and learning). For the motivational profile's mindsetType field, Dweck's research has direct implications for how the tutor responds to student errors and failures. Fixed mindset students interpret errors as evidence of permanent inability and require the tutor to explicitly reframe failure as a normal and necessary part of the learning process. Growth mindset students interpret errors as information and can be guided directly to the error without motivational scaffolding. The intake quiz must include mindset-probing scenarios: how does the student feel when they get a math problem wrong on the first try? A student who expresses embarrassment, frustration directed at themselves, or desire to stop trying is exhibiting fixed mindset indicators regardless of their stated belief. The tutor's response to a wrong answer must branch based on the mindsetType field: for fixed mindset students, the response leads with process acknowledgment before any mathematical redirection ("That's a really interesting attempt — let's see what happens when we look at this part first"). For growth mindset students, the tutor can move directly to the mathematical redirect without emotional scaffolding. Dweck's research also establishes that praising effort rather than intelligence is critical for developing growth mindset — the tutor's reinforcement language must always attribute success to the student's thinking process, never to innate ability.
