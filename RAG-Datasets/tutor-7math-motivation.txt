### Self-Determination Theory — The Three Core Psychological Needs in Math Learning
@@@meta:source https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/theory/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-001

Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan, identifies three universal psychological needs whose satisfaction drives intrinsic motivation and sustained engagement in any learning domain: autonomy (the need to experience one's actions as self-chosen and volitional), competence (the need to feel effective and capable within the learning environment), and relatedness (the need to feel connected to and valued by others in the learning context). Research across educational settings consistently demonstrates that learning environments satisfying all three needs produce higher intrinsic motivation, deeper learning, greater persistence, and better long-term retention than environments satisfying fewer than three. For an AI tutoring system, each need requires specific architectural support. Autonomy support: the tutor must provide choice at regular intervals — choice of problem context, choice of representation mode (graph vs. equation vs. table), choice of starting point within a concept. A student who experiences the tutoring session as something happening to them rather than something they are directing will develop extrinsic rather than intrinsic motivation, reducing long-term engagement. Autonomy support is implemented through choice-offering prompts built into the session structure: "would you rather start with a graph or an equation today?" and "which of these two problems would you like to tackle first?" Competence support: the tutor must maintain challenge calibration within the ZPD — problems too easy produce boredom and undermine competence perception, problems too hard produce failure experiences that damage competence perception. The mastery matrix's continuous tracking enables competence-supportive calibration. Relatedness support: the tutor must communicate genuine engagement with the student's specific thinking rather than generic responses — referencing the student's actual words and reasoning in each response makes the interaction feel relational rather than mechanical. The frictionMap's successfulWorkarounds and breakthroughPattern fields enable the tutor to reference the student's specific history, creating the experience of being known and remembered. All three needs must be actively supported in every session, not assumed to emerge from the content alone.

### Bandura's Self-Efficacy Theory — The Four Sources and Their Tutoring Implications
@@@meta:source https://www.uky.edu/~eushe2/Bandura/Bandura1977PR.pdf
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-002

Albert Bandura's self-efficacy theory identifies four sources through which domain-specific self-efficacy beliefs are built or eroded, each with distinct implications for AI tutoring design. Mastery experiences — direct personal successes in the domain — are the most powerful source of self-efficacy and the primary mechanism through which the tutor builds mathematical confidence. Each successful problem completion, each correct identification of slope, each self-corrected error represents a mastery experience contributing to the student's math self-efficacy. The tutor must engineer mastery experiences deliberately by calibrating problem difficulty to ensure regular success — a student who fails on every problem attempt regardless of hint support is accumulating efficacy-eroding failure experiences, not productive struggle. Vicarious experiences — observing similar others succeed — are the second source and are implemented in the tutor's Tier 3 worked parallel hint: watching a similar problem solved successfully provides vicarious experience that temporarily elevates efficacy for the attempt that follows. Research demonstrates that the worked parallel is most efficacy-building when the tutor frames it as "here is a student who had the same question — let's see how they figured it out" rather than "let me show you how this works," because peer framing activates vicarious learning more strongly than expert modeling. Verbal persuasion — credible encouragement — is the third source. The tutor's confirmation and reinforcement language builds efficacy only when it comes from a source the student perceives as credible and when it specifically attributes capability rather than luck. Physiological and emotional states — the student's anxiety, confidence, and arousal level — are the fourth source and the one most directly influenced by the math anxiety management protocols established in dataset tutor-7math-mathanxiety-001 through tutor-7math-mathanxiety-010. The motivational profile's selfEfficacyLevel field must be updated in the XML delta after each session based on behavioral indicators across all four sources.

### Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation — Protecting the Student's Internal Drive
@@@meta:source https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4255445/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-003

Deci and Ryan's foundational research on intrinsic and extrinsic motivation establishes the overjustification effect: when external rewards are introduced for activities a person was already intrinsically motivated to perform, intrinsic motivation decreases. For AI tutoring systems, this finding has a critical architectural implication — the tutor must not implement points systems, badges, leaderboards, or external reward structures, as these shift the student's motivational orientation from intrinsic (learning because understanding is satisfying) to extrinsic (learning to earn rewards), and when the rewards are removed the motivation collapses. For the Omega Logic MVP, this means no gamification elements beyond the inherent satisfaction of making progress. The motivational system must be entirely intrinsic-motivation-supporting: challenge calibrated to the student's ZPD produces intrinsic engagement through competence satisfaction, autonomy-supporting choice prompts produce intrinsic engagement through self-determination, and the tutor's genuine engagement with the student's thinking produces intrinsic engagement through relatedness. Research by Ryan and Deci further identifies that controlling feedback language — feedback that implicitly or explicitly pressures the student to perform correctly — erodes intrinsic motivation even when the feedback is positive. Controlling positive feedback: "you need to get this right," "I know you can do better," "you should know this by now." Autonomy-supportive positive feedback: "what approach feels most natural to you here," "take the direction that makes most sense to you," "your reasoning on the last step was solid — follow that same instinct." The tutor's language register must systematically use autonomy-supportive rather than controlling phrasing across all feedback types. The motivationalProfile's preferredReinforcementStyle field captures individual variation in how autonomy-supportive feedback should be expressed for each student.

### Goal Orientation Theory — Mastery vs. Performance Goals in Algebra Learning
@@@meta:source https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3843600/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-004

Goal Orientation Theory, developed by Carol Dweck and extended by Elliot and colleagues, distinguishes between mastery goals (oriented toward developing competence and understanding) and performance goals (oriented toward demonstrating competence relative to others or avoiding demonstrations of incompetence). Research consistently demonstrates that mastery goal orientation predicts deeper learning, greater persistence after failure, more effective use of learning strategies, and higher long-term achievement than performance goal orientation. For an AI tutoring system, goal orientation is a dynamic state that the tutor's language and structure can actively promote or inadvertently undermine. Mastery goal-promoting language focuses on understanding, process, and improvement: "let's figure out why this works," "what did you learn from that attempt," "you're getting more precise with slope identification." Performance goal-activating language focuses on correctness, comparison, and evaluation: "that's wrong," "you should have this," "most students get this right." The tutor must be explicitly programmed to use mastery goal-promoting language exclusively, never performance goal-activating language. Goal orientation interacts with mindset type from dataset tutor-7math-mathanxiety-010 in predictable ways: fixed mindset students are particularly vulnerable to performance goal activation because they interpret any performance feedback as evidence about their fixed ability. For fixed mindset students, mastery goal framing must be even more explicit — every session should include at least one direct statement connecting the student's effort to their developing understanding: "the fact that you caught that error yourself shows that your slope reasoning is getting sharper." Goal orientation should be tracked in the motivationalProfile and updated in the XML delta when sessions produce goal orientation shifts — a student beginning to ask "why does this work?" rather than "what's the answer?" is exhibiting a mastery goal orientation shift worth capturing.

### Situated Motivation — Why Context and Relevance Drive 7th Grade Math Engagement
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00461520.1991.9653136
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-005

Situated motivation theory, building on Vygotsky's emphasis on social and contextual factors in learning, holds that motivation is not a fixed trait of the student but a dynamic response to the specific learning situation encountered. A student who appears unmotivated in traditional algebra instruction may show high motivation when the same mathematical content is embedded in a personally relevant context. Research on situational interest — a temporary state of heightened engagement triggered by specific contextual features — identifies four features most reliably associated with situated interest in early adolescent mathematics: personal relevance (the problem connects to something the student cares about or experiences), novelty (the problem or approach is surprising or unexpected), challenge (the problem is at the edge of the student's capability, consistent with ZPD and flow theory), and social relevance (the problem involves real-world situations that affect people the student identifies with). For the Omega Logic tutor's 7th grade algebra context, situated interest is most reliably triggered by rate-of-change problems embedded in contexts adolescents encounter: social media growth rates, streaming subscription costs, athletic performance improvement over time, distance and speed in sports. The tutor engine must maintain a library of contextual problem framings for each Knowledge Component from dataset tutor-7math-hintsystems-008, and the system prompt must instruct the tutor to select contextual framings that match the student's engagementTriggers field from the motivationalProfile. The engagementTriggers field should be populated at intake through questions about the student's interests and activities, and updated through the XML delta when session observations reveal specific contextual framings that produced elevated engagement indicators (longer responses, spontaneous questions, forward-leaning language).

### Attribution Theory — How Students Explain Their Successes and Failures
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00461520.1984.9653438
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-006

Bernard Weiner's Attribution Theory identifies how students' causal explanations for their academic outcomes — their attributions — shape subsequent motivation and behavior. Attributions vary along three dimensions: locus (internal vs. external cause), stability (stable vs. unstable cause), and controllability (controllable vs. uncontrollable cause). Research identifies that the most motivation-sustaining attribution pattern for academic performance is internal-unstable-controllable: "I didn't succeed because I haven't practiced enough yet" (internal — my behavior, unstable — can change, controllable — within my power). The most motivation-destroying attribution pattern is internal-stable-uncontrollable: "I failed because I'm not smart enough" (internal — about me, stable — cannot change, uncontrollable — nothing I do matters). For 7th grade math students, the internal-stable-uncontrollable attribution ("I'm just bad at math") is the attribution form most closely associated with learned helplessness, fixed mindset, and long-term disengagement from mathematics. The tutor must actively counter this attribution pattern when it appears in student language — phrases like "I'm not a math person," "math doesn't make sense to my brain," or "I've never been good at this" are attribution statements requiring direct but gentle reframing. The reframing protocol: acknowledge the feeling without validating the attribution ("it makes sense that this feels hard — this is genuinely challenging material"), then redirect to an internal-unstable-controllable frame ("what we've found is that the students who get it are the ones who figure out their own approach — let's find yours"). The motivationalProfile's selfEfficacyLevel and mindsetType fields together predict which students are most vulnerable to negative attribution patterns, and the frictionMap's mathAnxietyIndicators should include attribution language as a specific indicator category.

### Expectancy-Value Theory — Predicting Task Engagement Before It Begins
@@@meta:source https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4233985/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-007

Expectancy-Value Theory, developed by Jacquelynne Eccles and colleagues, holds that a student's motivation to engage with a task is determined by two factors: their expectancy of success (do they believe they can succeed?) and their perceived value of the task (do they care about succeeding?). Both factors must be present for sustained motivation — high expectancy with low value produces capable but disengaged students, while high value with low expectancy produces anxious and frustrated students. For the Omega Logic tutor's intake process, Expectancy-Value Theory provides the framework for two critical intake assessments. Expectancy assessment: questions that reveal the student's prior success history in mathematics and their current confidence in their mathematical capability, feeding the selfEfficacyLevel field. Value assessment: questions that reveal why the student is using the tutor, what they hope to get from it, and whether they see mathematics as relevant to their life and goals, feeding a valueOrientation field that should be added to the motivationalProfile. Research identifies four components of perceived task value relevant to middle school mathematics: intrinsic value (do they find math inherently interesting?), attainment value (is being good at math important to their identity?), utility value (do they see math as useful for their goals?), and cost (what are they giving up by spending time on math?). The tutor's session opening should include brief autonomy-supportive check-ins that reinforce the student's value connection: "what are you working toward with math?" and "how does getting better at this connect to what you care about?" These prompts are not mere rapport-building but evidence-based interventions that reinforce the value component of the expectancy-value equation, sustaining engagement through the difficult middle sessions where novelty has worn off but mastery has not yet arrived.

### Motivation Across Sessions — The Engagement Trajectory Problem in EdTech
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10508406.2016.1146449
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-008

Research on engagement trajectories in educational technology products identifies a consistent three-phase pattern that the Omega Logic tutor must be designed to address across the full one-month MVP deployment. Phase one — novelty engagement (sessions 1-3): students engage primarily because the AI tutor is new and interesting. Motivation is high but fragile — it is driven by situational interest in the technology rather than the content, and will collapse if the content experience is not satisfying. During this phase, the tutor must prioritize success experiences and relationship-building over mathematical challenge, ensuring the student's first impression of the tutor is as a capable ally rather than a source of difficulty. Phase two — motivational trough (sessions 4-8): novelty has worn off and genuine mastery has not yet arrived — the student is working hard but does not yet feel competent. Research identifies this as the highest dropout-risk period in EdTech products, with 40-60% of users disengaging during this window. The tutor must be proactively designed to address the trough: explicit progress visualization ("look at the slope problems you could not do in session 1 — here is one of them, try it now"), increased autonomy support to maintain self-determination, and deliberate mastery experience engineering through problem selection from the mastery matrix. Phase three — mastery engagement (sessions 9+): students who reach demonstrable competence on core concepts experience a significant motivation boost from the competence satisfaction identified in SDT. The tutor's challenge calibration must advance ahead of the student's growing mastery to maintain the flow state documented in dataset tutor-7math-mathanxiety-008, preventing the session from becoming too easy and triggering boredom disengagement. The XML delta's motivationalUpdates block should include a sessionEngagementLevel field (high/medium/low based on response quality, length, and initiative) that tracks engagement trajectory across sessions and triggers phase-appropriate motivational interventions.

### The Role of Curiosity in Mathematical Motivation — Epistemic Emotions
@@@meta:source https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6107969/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-009

Research on epistemic emotions — emotions specifically triggered by knowledge acquisition and gap detection — identifies curiosity as a primary driver of intrinsic motivation in learning contexts. Curiosity is triggered by information gaps: the awareness that one does not know something one wants to know. For mathematics tutoring, curiosity can be deliberately activated through question design. Prediction questions activate curiosity by creating an information gap the student immediately wants to fill: "before we calculate, what do you predict will happen to the y-value if x doubles?" The student who makes a prediction is now motivated to find the correct answer not merely for compliance but to resolve their own curiosity about whether they predicted correctly. Paradox questions activate curiosity by presenting a surprising or counterintuitive situation: "here are two equations — y = 2x + 100 and y = 10x + 1. Which line do you think will have a higher y-value when x = 20? Your intuition might surprise you." The surprise resolution — discovering that a small slope advantage compounds dramatically over large x values — is inherently satisfying and more memorable than the same information delivered without the curiosity activation sequence. For the Omega Logic tutor engine, curiosity activation should be a deliberate session design element, particularly during the motivational trough period identified in dataset tutor-7math-motivation-008. The tutor's system prompt should include explicit instruction to deploy prediction questions at session opening and paradox questions at the midpoint of each session, with the specific predictions and paradoxes tailored to the student's current position in the concept sequence and their engagementTriggers profile field. Sessions that include at least one curiosity-activating sequence show measurably higher engagement duration and deeper exploratory reasoning in student responses.

### Long-Term Motivational Sustainability — Building a Mathematical Identity
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10508406.2015.1128199
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-motivation-010

Research on mathematical identity — the extent to which a student sees mathematical competence as part of their self-concept — identifies it as the strongest predictor of long-term mathematics engagement beyond early adolescence. Students who develop a mathematical identity by the end of middle school are significantly more likely to pursue advanced mathematics coursework, persist through difficulty in high school algebra and beyond, and apply mathematical reasoning spontaneously in novel contexts. Mathematical identity is built through accumulated experiences of mathematical competence and recognition — the student not only succeeds but comes to see themselves as someone who succeeds at mathematics. For the Omega Logic tutor, mathematical identity development is the ultimate long-term motivational goal — more important than any individual session's mastery score. Three specific tutoring practices have been empirically linked to mathematical identity development in middle school students. Competence narration: the tutor explicitly narrates the student's developing competence over time — "three sessions ago you couldn't identify slope from a graph. Today you did it without any hints. That's your mathematical reasoning growing." This narration requires the cumulative session data from the mastery matrix and frictionMap to be accurate and specific. Agency attribution: the tutor consistently attributes the student's mathematical progress to their own choices, strategies, and effort rather than to the tutor's explanations or the curriculum's structure — "you figured that out by deciding to check the x-coefficient first. That was your strategy, and it worked." This attribution directly supports the development of an agentive mathematical identity. Community membership language: the tutor occasionally uses inclusive mathematical community language that positions the student as a developing member of a group of mathematical thinkers — "mathematicians always check their work by substituting back in — let's do what mathematicians do." This subtle identity invitation, used sparingly, gradually builds the student's self-perception as a mathematical thinker rather than a struggling student.
