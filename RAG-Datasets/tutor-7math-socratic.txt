### Classical Socratic Method Applied to Mathematics — Core Principles and Adaptations
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220270601041307
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-001

The Socratic method, originating in Plato's dialogues, operates through a structured sequence of questions designed to expose contradictions in a student's existing beliefs, guide them to recognize their own ignorance, and lead them through self-discovery to accurate understanding. Applied to mathematics, the method requires three fundamental adaptations from its philosophical origins. First, mathematical Socratic questioning must be constructive rather than purely deconstructive — while the classical method focuses on exposing what the student does not know, mathematical Socratic questioning must simultaneously build toward a specific, correct understanding rather than leaving the student in a state of productive confusion indefinitely. Second, mathematical questions must be answerable through the student's existing knowledge base — a Socratic question that requires knowledge the student has not yet acquired is not productive challenge but a setup for failure. This maps directly to the Vygotsky ZPD constraint established in dataset tutor-7math-learningstyles-002: every Socratic question must be answerable from within the student's current cognitive reach. Third, mathematical Socratic questioning requires explicit handling of incorrect answers — unlike philosophical dialogue where any thoughtful response advances understanding, mathematics has correct and incorrect answers, and the Socratic method must guide incorrect answers toward self-correction without direct negation. The fundamental principle for handling incorrect mathematical answers Socratically is never to say "that's wrong" but instead to ask a question that makes the incorrectness self-evident: "if the slope were 5, what would y equal when x is 1? Does that match what the equation gives you?" This technique, called productive verification prompting, turns the incorrect answer into a testable hypothesis the student can refute themselves.

### Bloom's Question Taxonomy Applied to Algebra — Mapping Question Types to Cognitive Levels
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220671.1969.10883974
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-002

Bloom's Taxonomy (established in dataset tutor-7math-learningstyles-001) provides a direct framework for categorizing and sequencing Socratic questions in algebra tutoring. Each cognitive level maps to a distinct question type that the tutor must deploy in level-appropriate sequence — asking an Evaluation-level question to a student still at the Remember level produces the cognitive equivalent of asking someone to critique a piece of music they have never heard. Remember-level questions target recall of definitions and procedures: "What does m represent in y = mx + b?" "What is the formula for slope using two points?" These questions are appropriate only for establishing baseline recall and should not be used as the primary questioning mode because they do not develop understanding. Understand-level questions target comprehension of meaning: "Why does a larger m make the line steeper?" "What does it mean for two points to satisfy the same equation?" These are the primary entry point for Socratic dialogue with students in the early stages of slope-intercept instruction. Apply-level questions target transfer to new instances: "Using what you know about slope, can you predict whether this line goes up or down from left to right?" "Without calculating, which of these two lines is steeper?" Analyze-level questions target structural examination: "What would change about the line if we doubled m but kept b the same?" "How are these two equations similar and how are they different?" Evaluate-level questions target judgment with justification: "Which representation — the equation or the graph — gives you more information about this line, and why?" The tutor must track which cognitive level the student is currently operating at in the mastery matrix and select questions from the appropriate level, never more than one level above the student's demonstrated competence.

### Funneling vs. Focusing Question Sequences — The Critical Distinction for AI Tutors
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/749608
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-003

Research by Herbel-Eisenmann and Breyfogle identifies a critical distinction between two questioning patterns that superficially resemble each other but produce fundamentally different learning outcomes: funneling sequences and focusing sequences. A funneling sequence occurs when the teacher has a specific answer in mind and asks a series of progressively narrower questions designed to lead the student to that predetermined answer — the student's thinking is not genuinely engaged but is being guided along a predetermined path. A focusing sequence occurs when the teacher's questions genuinely attend to the student's expressed thinking and respond to it, building the next question from what the student actually said rather than from the predetermined answer path. Funneling produces correct answers in the short term but does not develop mathematical reasoning because the student learns to follow conversational cues rather than to think independently. Focusing produces slower short-term progress but builds genuine mathematical reasoning and metacognitive development. For an AI tutoring system, this distinction is architecturally critical: a tutor that generates questions based solely on the correct answer path (what the student should think) rather than on what the student actually expressed (what the student does think) is implementing funneling, not Socratic dialogue. The tutor engine must generate its next question by first parsing the student's actual response — identifying what the student's answer reveals about their current understanding — and then selecting a question that engages with that specific expressed understanding. This requires the tutor's system prompt to explicitly instruct: respond to what the student said, not to what you expected them to say. The frictionMap's triggerPattern field should capture instances where the student's thinking diverged significantly from the expected path, as these are the highest-value data points for understanding the student's actual cognitive model.

### Wait Time Research — Response Latency as a Socratic Tool
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/749379
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-004

Mary Budd Rowe's foundational research on wait time demonstrates that extending the pause after asking a question from less than 1 second (the typical teacher pause) to 3-5 seconds produces dramatic improvements in student response quality: longer responses, more complex reasoning, more student-initiated questions, and higher rates of student-to-student connection of ideas. In a text-based AI tutoring system, wait time is not directly controllable — the student determines their own response latency. However, the research has two applicable implications for the tutor's behavior. First, the tutor must never rush the student with follow-up prompts before the student has responded — sending a second hint or question before the student replies is the text-based equivalent of reducing wait time, signaling that the tutor does not expect the student to produce a thoughtful response. If the student's response to a Socratic question is notably brief or incomplete, the tutor's first response should be a wait time extension prompt rather than a new hint: "Take your time — what's your initial instinct about this?" or "What's the first thing that comes to mind when you look at this equation?" Second, the quality of the student's response is a diagnostic signal: a very brief response ("I don't know," "idk," single-word answers) after a Socratic question that the student's mastery matrix indicates they should be able to engage with suggests either anxiety suppression (see dataset tutor-7math-mathanxiety-002) or insufficient wait time self-tolerance — the student has learned to respond immediately rather than thinking before responding. The tutor should explicitly give permission for extended thinking: "There's no rush here — think it through before you answer."

### Probing Questions — Deepening Understanding Beyond Surface Correct Answers
@@@meta:source https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4016739/
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-005

Probing questions are Socratic questions posed in response to a correct answer, designed to test whether the correct answer reflects genuine understanding or surface pattern-matching. Research in mathematics education identifies that a significant proportion of correct answers in early algebra are produced through template application (recognizing the form and filling in the blanks) rather than through conceptual understanding — and these template-correct answers are indistinguishable from understanding-correct answers without probing. The mastery update rule established in dataset tutor-7math-metacognition-002 (3:1 weighting for independent vs. hint-supported performance) must be further refined for probing: only answers that survive probing questions should receive full mastery score weighting. Five categories of probing questions for 7th grade algebra: Justification probing — "Why does that work?" "How do you know that's the slope?" Generalization probing — "Would the same approach work if m were a fraction?" "What if b were negative?" Counterexample probing — "Can you think of a situation where that rule wouldn't apply?" "What would have to be different about the equation for your answer to change?" Elaboration probing — "Can you explain that in a different way?" "How would you show that to someone who had never seen this before?" Connection probing — "How does this connect to what you know about proportions?" "Where have you seen something like this before?" The tutor must deploy at least one probing question for every correct answer the student produces without hint support, before updating the mastery matrix. This ensures that mastery scores reflect genuine understanding rather than pattern-matching fluency.

### Questioning Sequences for Common Algebra Misconceptions — Targeted Socratic Protocols
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/748884
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-006

Research on targeted questioning sequences for specific algebra misconceptions identifies that effective Socratic correction of a misconception requires a three-stage question sequence: destabilization, reconstruction, and verification. Destabilization questions create productive cognitive conflict by revealing an internal contradiction in the student's current understanding without directly stating that the understanding is wrong. For slope-intercept reversal (dataset tutor-7math-algebraerrors-001): "You said m is the y-intercept. If I change the value of m in the equation, what would you expect to change about the graph?" When the student predicts a y-intercept change but the graph shows a slope change, the contradiction is self-evident without the tutor having corrected anything. Reconstruction questions guide the student to build the correct understanding from the contradiction they have just observed: "Now that we've seen what changes when we adjust m — what does m actually control about the line?" Verification questions confirm the reconstructed understanding transfers to a new instance: "Using what you just discovered, can you tell me what will change about this new line if I increase m from 2 to 5?" This three-stage sequence (destabilize, reconstruct, verify) must be applied to each of the systematic error types documented in dataset tutor-7math-algebraerrors-001 through tutor-7math-algebraerrors-010. The specific destabilization question must be tailored to each error type's cognitive origin — generic destabilization questions that do not target the specific misconception's internal contradiction are ineffective. The tutor engine's system prompt must include the full three-stage sequence for each documented error type, and the tutor must detect which error type is active before selecting the appropriate sequence.

### Open vs. Closed Questions — Balancing Convergent and Divergent Thinking in Math
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/0020739X.2016.1160 
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-007

Mathematics education research distinguishes between closed questions (those with a single correct answer) and open questions (those with multiple valid responses or approaches). Both question types serve distinct pedagogical functions in Socratic tutoring, and an effective tutoring session requires deliberate sequencing of both. Closed questions establish factual anchors and provide unambiguous checkpoints for mastery assessment: "What is the slope of y = 4x - 7?" has exactly one correct answer and allows the tutor to precisely locate the student on the mastery matrix. Open questions develop mathematical reasoning and reveal the depth of understanding beyond factual recall: "How would you describe the difference between a steep line and a gradual line to someone who has never seen a graph?" has multiple valid formulations and reveals whether the student has a conceptual model or only a procedural rule. Research identifies that effective math tutoring sessions should begin with closed questions to establish cognitive anchors, transition to open questions to develop understanding, and return to closed questions for verification — a sandwich structure. The ratio of open to closed questions should increase as the student's mastery level increases: at mastery level 0.3-0.5, approximately 70% closed and 30% open; at mastery level 0.5-0.75, approximately 50/50; at mastery level above 0.75, approximately 30% closed and 70% open. This ratio adjustment reflects the shift from knowledge-building (requiring accurate closed-question anchors) to understanding-development (requiring open-question exploration). The tutor engine must track the current mastery level at the start of each session and calibrate the question-type ratio accordingly.

### Hypothetical and Counterfactual Questions — Building Algebraic Reasoning Through Imagination
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220671.2008.10599052
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-008

Hypothetical and counterfactual questions — "what would happen if...?" and "what would be different if...?" — are among the most powerful Socratic tools for developing algebraic reasoning in early adolescence. Research demonstrates that these question types activate formal operational reasoning (the Piaget stage documented in dataset tutor-7math-cognition-003) more reliably than direct instruction because they engage the student's imagination as the primary cognitive tool, reducing the perceived stakes of being wrong. A counterfactual question ("what would the graph look like if the slope were zero?") invites the student to reason about a hypothetical case that illuminates the general principle — slope zero means no change in y, meaning a horizontal line — through imaginative exploration rather than rule application. This approach is particularly effective for students with high abstractionTolerance ratings because it leverages their capacity for hypothetical reasoning directly. For students with low abstractionTolerance ratings who require concrete anchors, hypothetical questions must be grounded before they become imaginative: "we've drawn the line for y = 2x + 1. Now imagine erasing that line and drawing y = 0x + 1 instead. What's different about how y changes as x increases?" The grounding (starting from a concrete drawn line) makes the hypothetical manipulation of slope accessible before the abstract zero-slope concept is introduced. Specific high-value hypothetical question patterns for y = mx + b instruction: slope manipulation hypotheticals ("what happens to the line as m gets larger and larger?"), intercept manipulation hypotheticals ("if b increases by 5, where does the new line appear relative to the old one?"), and sign change hypotheticals ("what changes when m becomes negative?"). Each of these question types directly addresses a core concept in linear equations while engaging the student's reasoning rather than their recall.

### Error Correction Through Questioning — Never Telling, Always Asking
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/749439
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-009

Research on Socratic error correction in mathematics establishes a complete protocol for handling incorrect student answers through questioning rather than direct correction. The protocol has five sequential steps, each implemented as a question rather than a statement. Step one — acknowledge without evaluating: respond to the incorrect answer with a neutral acknowledgment that neither confirms nor denies correctness: "Okay, you're thinking the slope is 4. Let's explore that." This prevents the defensive response that direct correction triggers and keeps the student cognitively engaged. Step two — request reasoning: "Can you walk me through how you got 4?" Requesting reasoning serves two functions: it gives the student the opportunity to self-correct during explanation (which happens frequently and should be acknowledged as evidence of metacognitive development), and it reveals the specific cognitive step where the error originated, enabling targeted questioning in step three. Step three — target the error origin: based on the reasoning the student provided, ask a question that specifically addresses the step where the reasoning diverged from correct thinking. If the student correctly identified m but misread its sign, the targeting question addresses sign interpretation specifically: "You said m is 4 — let me look at the equation with you. Which part of the equation represents m?" Step four — guide to self-correction: ask a question that allows the student to arrive at the correct answer from their own revised reasoning: "Given what you just said about which term is m, what value does it have?" Step five — confirm the correction belongs to the student: "So what's the slope?" Asking the student to restate the correct answer after self-correction ensures they own the correction rather than attributing it to the tutor's guidance. This final ownership confirmation is critical for self-efficacy development.

### Socratic Questioning Across Cognitive Fingerprint Types — Adapting the Method to the Student
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220671.2018.1514113
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-010

Research on differentiated Socratic questioning establishes that the optimal question formulation varies significantly across student cognitive profiles, and that a one-size-fits-all questioning approach produces systematically unequal learning outcomes. For the tutor engine's cognitive fingerprint, each primaryLearningMode requires adapted question formulations that route the same mathematical content through the student's dominant processing channel. For visual-spatial learners (Gardner Spatial intelligence, concrete abstractionTolerance): Socratic questions should reference graphical representations as the primary reasoning vehicle — "looking at the graph, what do you notice about what happens to the line as you move from left to right?" "If you were going to draw this equation, where would you start?" For algebraic-logical learners (Gardner Logical-Mathematical, abstract abstractionTolerance): Socratic questions should engage formal reasoning structures — "what does the equation tell you algebraically about the relationship between x and y?" "If you substitute x = 0 into the equation, what must be true?" For narrative learners (Gardner Linguistic, semi-abstract abstractionTolerance): Socratic questions should embed mathematical concepts in contextual stories — "imagine you're tracking how far a car has traveled over time — what would slope mean in that story?" "If you were explaining this graph to a friend using words, what story would it tell?" For failure-first processors (Kolb Accommodator, growth mindset): Socratic questions should invite deliberate exploration and prediction-testing — "before you calculate, what do you predict will happen? Now let's check." For sequential processors (Kolb Assimilator): Socratic questions should explicitly sequence each step — "what's the very first thing you need to identify before you can find the slope?" The tutor must retrieve the student's primaryLearningMode from the cognitive fingerprint at session start and maintain question formulation consistency throughout the session, switching modes only when the current mode shows sustained failure to engage the student.

### Question Sequencing for a Complete Tutoring Session — Opening, Development, and Closure
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220671.2009.10599153
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-socratic-011

Research on effective tutoring session structure identifies that question sequencing across a complete session — not just within individual problem-solving episodes — significantly affects both learning outcomes and student engagement. A complete Socratic tutoring session for 7th grade algebra should follow a three-phase questioning arc. Phase one — session opening (first 3-5 exchanges): questions serve two functions simultaneously. Retrieval practice questions warm up prior knowledge from the mastery matrix — "before we start today's new concept, let me ask you about something from last time: what's the slope of y = 3x + 7?" — and establish the emotional tone for the session through engagement-level calibration — "how are you feeling about algebra today?" The retrieval question should target a concept with mastery score above 0.6 to ensure a success experience before new material is introduced (consistent with dataset tutor-7math-mathanxiety-004's success sequencing strategy). Phase two — session development (the main instructional block): questions progress through Bloom's levels from Understand through Analyze, with question type ratio calibrated to mastery level as established in dataset tutor-7math-socratic-007. Error correction sequences from dataset tutor-7math-socratic-009 are deployed as needed. Hypothetical and counterfactual questions (dataset tutor-7math-socratic-008) are introduced in the middle of this phase when the student has established sufficient conceptual grounding. Phase three — session closure (final 3-5 exchanges): reflection questions anchor the session's learning in long-term memory — "what's the most important thing you figured out today?" — and metacognitive regulation questions build self-assessment habits — "what would you do differently if you saw a similar problem tomorrow?" The closing phase must always end with a forward-looking statement that maintains the student's sense of mathematical trajectory and counters the session-end emotional drop that research identifies in math-anxious students.
