### Slope-Intercept Reversal — The Most Common Systematic Error in y = mx + b
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/749137
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-001

Slope-intercept reversal is the single most frequently documented systematic error in 7th grade linear equation instruction: the student consistently assigns b as the slope and m as the y-intercept, reversing the roles of the two parameters. Research across multiple large-scale studies identifies this error in approximately 35-45% of students during initial instruction on slope-intercept form. The error is not random — it is a systematic misconception with an identifiable cognitive origin. Students learning y = mx + b encounter the equation left to right: y, then m, then x, then b. The y-intercept is conceptually the "starting point" of the line, and students with gestalt-first processing styles frequently anchor on b as the first meaningful number they encounter when reading the equation in isolation from its graph, treating positional prominence rather than algebraic role as the identification rule. Additionally, students who are introduced to the equation verbally before graphically may encode "the line starts at b and goes up by m" but reverse the labels because the spatial description (starting point, then slope) maps onto the equation's symbol order (m, then b) in the wrong direction. Detection in tutor sessions: the student correctly identifies that one parameter controls steepness and the other controls vertical position but assigns them to the wrong symbols. Correction strategy: do not restate the rule. Instead, use the graph-anchoring technique — ask the student to graph the equation with their (reversed) values and observe that the line does not cross the y-axis at the value they identified as the intercept. The visual contradiction between their prediction and the graph creates cognitive dissonance that resolves the misconception more durably than rule restatement. The frictionMap entry for this error should log whether graph-anchoring or direct rule correction was used first and which produced resolution.

### Sign Error on Negative Slope — Magnitude Without Direction
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220671.1988.10885843
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-002

Sign errors on negative slope represent the second most common systematic error in y = mx + b instruction: the student correctly identifies the magnitude of the slope but consistently drops or ignores the negative sign, treating m = -3 as equivalent to m = 3 in calculations and graph interpretation. This error has a distinct cognitive origin from slope-intercept reversal. Negative number handling is itself a significant cognitive challenge for 7th grade students who are simultaneously developing integer arithmetic fluency, and the additional layer of a negative slope requiring the student to move down the y-axis while moving right on the x-axis violates the intuitive spatial expectation that "moving forward" (increasing x) corresponds to "going up" (increasing y). The sign error frequently coexists with a directional error: the student draws the line going upward left-to-right even when slope is negative, because the magnitude of rise is correctly calculated but the direction is reversed. Detection in tutor sessions: the student correctly substitutes m's numerical value into calculations but produces answers consistent with the absolute value of m rather than its signed value, or describes the line as "going up 3 for every 1 right" when m = -3. The student may even write the negative sign in the equation but not apply it in their reasoning. Correction strategy: the slope-as-direction prompt sequence — "if I start at the y-intercept and move one step to the right, which direction does the negative sign tell me to move?" — addresses the directional interpretation before the numerical calculation. For spatial learners, physically tracing the line direction on a graph while verbalizing "right and down" builds the motor-spatial encoding that overrides the intuitive "right and up" default. This error frequently resolves faster for spatial learners than for algebraic learners because the visual-spatial representation makes the direction violation immediately obvious.

### Variable Confusion — Treating x and y as Fixed Values Rather Than Varying Quantities
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/748861
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-003

Variable confusion is a foundational algebraic misconception in which the student treats x and y as fixed, specific unknown values (as in arithmetic equation solving: find the value of x) rather than as co-varying quantities that define a relationship across infinitely many values. This misconception, extensively documented by Küchemann and subsequent researchers, produces a characteristic error pattern: the student attempts to "solve" y = mx + b for a single numerical answer rather than recognizing it as a description of all coordinate pairs satisfying the relationship. In practical tutoring sessions, this manifests as the student asking "but what does x equal?" when presented with y = 2x + 3, or successfully calculating y for a given x but being unable to explain why multiple (x,y) pairs all satisfy the same equation. The misconception represents a categorical conceptual gap rather than a procedural error and requires fundamental reframing before any slope or intercept work can proceed. Detection: the student's language treats the equation as a puzzle to be solved rather than a relationship to be described. Correction strategy: the tutor must not scaffold slope-intercept content for a student exhibiting active variable confusion — attempting to teach slope identification before variable understanding is resolved builds on a broken foundation. The prerequisite remediation sequence begins with concrete co-variation examples outside of algebra: "if a movie ticket costs $12, and you buy x tickets, the total cost y changes as x changes — can you tell me what y is when x is 3? When x is 5? When x is 0?" This builds the co-variation concept experientially before reintroducing formal equation notation. The mastery matrix must flag variable understanding as a prerequisite gate concept with mastery threshold 0.75 required before slope-intercept instruction can begin.

### Graph-to-Equation Translation Errors — Extracting Parameters from Visual Representations
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/749914
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-004

Graph-to-equation translation errors occur when a student can correctly identify slope and y-intercept from an equation but cannot extract the same parameters from a graphical representation, or vice versa. Research identifies this as a bidirectional failure: some students can move from equation to graph (they can draw the line from y = 2x + 3) but cannot move from graph to equation (they cannot write y = 2x + 3 from a drawn line). Others show the reverse pattern. The bidirectional translation between algebraic and graphical representations of linear equations is a distinct cognitive skill that must be explicitly taught and assessed separately from single-direction competence. Equation-to-graph failures typically involve correct slope application but y-intercept placement error — the student starts drawing from the origin (0,0) rather than from the y-intercept (0,b), a procedural error rooted in the habit of treating the origin as the default starting point from coordinate plane work. Graph-to-equation failures typically involve rise-over-run calculation errors: the student correctly identifies the direction of the slope (positive or negative) but miscounts the grid units when calculating the ratio, particularly when the slope is a fraction (e.g., reading a slope of 2/3 as 3/2 by inverting rise and run). Correction strategy for equation-to-graph: the tutor's first prompt should establish starting point before slope: "before you draw the line, where will it cross the y-axis?" For graph-to-equation: the tutor should prompt deliberate labeling of two clearly identifiable points before the rise-over-run calculation, reducing the working memory load of the counting procedure. The frictionMap should record which direction of translation is impaired for each student, as the correction strategies diverge completely.

### Slope as a Point Rather Than a Rate — The Static Interpretation Error
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10986065.2012.681543
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-005

A significant proportion of 7th grade students develop a static rather than dynamic interpretation of slope: they can identify that m = 3 in y = 3x + 5 but conceptualize 3 as a fixed property of the line (like its color or length) rather than as a rate describing how y changes relative to x. This static interpretation produces a characteristic failure: the student cannot apply slope to calculate y for an arbitrary x value because they do not understand slope as a multiplicative relationship between x-change and y-change. The static interpretation is detectable through specific question types: "if x increases by 2, what happens to y?" A student with dynamic slope understanding immediately answers "y increases by 6 (2 times the slope of 3)." A student with static interpretation cannot answer this question or defaults to substituting x = 2 into the full equation rather than reasoning proportionally about the rate. This error is particularly consequential because static slope interpretation blocks all downstream applications of slope including rate-of-change word problems, parallel and perpendicular line reasoning, and later work with functions and derivatives. Correction strategy: the rate-of-change reframing sequence — "slope tells us how many steps y takes for every single step x takes" — followed by a table-building exercise where the student explicitly records y values as x increases by 1, 2, 3, identifying the constant additive pattern. The table makes the dynamic relationship concrete and countable before the abstract rate interpretation is introduced. For students whose frictionMap shows resistance to the rate framing, a real-world anchoring ("a car moving at 3 miles per minute — how far has it gone after 4 minutes? After x minutes?") accesses the proportional reasoning schema that the student likely has from arithmetic contexts and bridges it to algebraic slope.

### Y-Intercept as Origin Confusion — Defaulting to (0,0) as the Starting Point
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220671.1993.9941208
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-006

Y-intercept confusion is a systematic error in which the student consistently treats the y-intercept as the origin (0,0) rather than the point (0,b), defaulting to the assumption that all lines begin at the origin unless explicitly told otherwise. This error has a clear developmental origin: prior to linear equation instruction, virtually all graphing activities in elementary and middle school involve proportional relationships (y = mx with b = 0), where lines do pass through the origin. The student has developed an overgeneralization from proportional to non-proportional linear relationships, treating the origin-crossing as a default property of all lines rather than a special case. Detection in tutoring sessions: the student correctly applies slope to draw a line but places it through the origin regardless of b's value, or when asked to identify the y-intercept of a graphed line, consistently answers "zero" or points to the origin. The student may correctly state "b is the y-intercept" but not connect this to the actual point (0,b) on the graph. Correction strategy: the cover-the-slope technique — ask the student to temporarily ignore the mx term and consider what the equation says about y when x equals exactly zero: "if x is zero, what does y = 3(0) + 5 simplify to?" This calculation directly produces y = 5, making the y-intercept value concrete without requiring spatial reasoning about graph placement. After confirming the calculation, the graph anchor — "so where on the y-axis does y equal 5?" — connects the algebraic value to the graphical position. This two-step sequence separates the algebraic calculation from the graphical placement, reducing the simultaneous cognitive load of both operations.

### Equation Rearrangement Errors — Failing to Identify Slope-Intercept Form
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/749136
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-007

Equation rearrangement errors occur when a linear equation is presented in a non-standard form (e.g., 2x + y = 7, or 3x - 2y = 6) and the student cannot identify slope and y-intercept without first rearranging to slope-intercept form, or attempts to identify slope and intercept directly from the non-standard form using slope-intercept rules incorrectly. This error type is particularly common when students have memorized the slope-intercept form as a visual template (y = mx + b means "the number before x is slope, the last number is intercept") rather than understanding the underlying algebraic structure. When this template is applied to 2x + y = 7, the student may identify 2 as slope and 7 as intercept without recognizing that neither identification is valid in this form. Research indicates that students who have developed only template-based identification of slope and intercept — rather than algebraic understanding of what slope and intercept mean — are highly vulnerable to this error. Correction strategy: the tutor must establish whether the student understands slope-intercept form conceptually or only as a visual template before introducing non-standard forms. The diagnostic question — "why does putting the equation in the form y = mx + b make it easier to read the slope?" — reveals whether the student understands that isolating y is the prerequisite for slope-intercept identification. Students who cannot answer this question require conceptual grounding in equation isolation before rearrangement practice. For students who understand conceptually but make procedural errors in rearrangement, the error is typically a sign error during the subtraction step, requiring targeted practice on algebraic manipulation with integers rather than slope-intercept concept remediation.

### Proportional vs Non-Proportional Confusion — When Lines Do Not Pass Through the Origin
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10986065.2018.1442097
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-008

A foundational conceptual confusion in early algebra is the failure to distinguish proportional relationships (y = mx, where b = 0 and the line passes through the origin) from non-proportional linear relationships (y = mx + b, where b ≠ 0). Students who conflate these categories apply proportional reasoning strategies to non-proportional problems, producing systematic errors. The most common manifestation: given a table of values for a non-proportional relationship, the student attempts to identify slope by computing y/x ratios (the correct strategy for proportional relationships) rather than computing the constant difference in y-values per unit change in x. When b ≠ 0, y/x ratios change across the table even when the slope is constant, leading the student to incorrectly conclude that the relationship has no constant slope or is non-linear. Detection: the student correctly identifies slope in equation form but cannot identify slope from a table of values for a non-proportional relationship, or states that the table "doesn't have a pattern" despite a constant slope. Correction strategy: the two-column comparison exercise — present the student with two tables side by side, one proportional and one non-proportional with the same slope — and ask them to compute both y/x ratios and y-differences across both tables. The visual contrast between the constant y/x ratios in the proportional table and the varying y/x ratios in the non-proportional table, paired with the constant y-differences in both tables, makes the structural distinction concrete. The key conceptual message — "slope lives in the differences, not the ratios, when the line doesn't start at zero" — bridges the student's existing proportional reasoning to the non-proportional case without invalidating their prior knowledge.

### Fraction Slope Errors — Misapplying Rise Over Run with Non-Integer Slopes
@@@meta:source https://www.jstor.org/stable/748779
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-009

Fraction slope errors occur when the slope m is a non-integer rational number (e.g., m = 2/3, m = -3/4) and the student makes systematic errors in both interpreting and applying the fractional value. Research identifies three distinct error patterns within this category. Inversion error: the student consistently inverts the slope fraction, applying run/rise rather than rise/run, treating 2/3 slope as though it were 3/2. This error frequently coexists with run-first spatial processing, where the student's habitual scanning direction (horizontal before vertical) maps the denominator onto rise and the numerator onto run. Decimal conversion error: the student correctly identifies the fraction but converts it to a decimal imprecisely before applying it (2/3 becomes 0.6 or 0.66 rather than the exact fraction), producing rounding errors that compound through multi-step calculations. Whole number bias: the student recognizes that the slope should be 2/3 but applies it as though it were the whole number 2, treating the denominator as insignificant. This error is associated with students whose fraction fluency from prior instruction is low and who are simultaneously managing both fraction arithmetic and slope application for the first time. Correction strategy varies by error type. For inversion: the labeled rise-run scaffold — ask the student to explicitly label "rise = up/down movement" and "run = left/right movement" before any calculation, then verify which movement corresponds to the numerator before applying. For decimal conversion: insist on fraction-form slope throughout the problem, introducing decimal conversion only at the final step if required. For whole number bias: return to the co-variation table with x-increments matching the denominator (increment x by 3 when slope is 2/3) so the whole-number rise becomes visible before the fractional notation is introduced.

### Word Problem Translation Errors — Moving from Context to Equation
@@@meta:source https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00220671.1995.9941802
@@@meta:external_id tutor-7math-algebraerrors-010

Word problem translation errors occur at the boundary between contextual understanding and algebraic representation: the student understands the real-world scenario described in the problem but cannot correctly map its components onto y = mx + b. Research identifies this as a two-stage failure process. At stage one, the student misidentifies which quantity in the problem corresponds to slope (the rate of change) and which corresponds to y-intercept (the starting value). Common confusion patterns: in a problem describing a taxi fare (base charge plus per-mile rate), students frequently assign the base charge to m and the per-mile rate to b, because the base charge is numerically larger and feels more "important." At stage two, students who correctly identify the real-world slope and intercept fail to maintain the correct units through the equation, treating m as a pure number rather than a rate with units (dollars per mile) and b as a pure number rather than a value with units (dollars). This unit-stripping produces equations that are algebraically correct but contextually meaningless, and the student cannot use them to answer contextual questions ("how much does a 7-mile trip cost?") without re-reading the original problem. Correction strategy: the rate-vs-starting-value identification prompt — "in this situation, what changes as the situation develops, and what stays constant from the beginning?" — guides the student to identify rate (slope) as the quantity that changes and starting value (intercept) as the quantity that is fixed at the outset, without referencing algebraic symbols initially. Once the real-world roles are correctly identified, the symbol assignment follows directly. For unit maintenance, the tutor should require the student to write units alongside every number throughout the problem-solving process, treating unit-free equations as incomplete until units are restored.
